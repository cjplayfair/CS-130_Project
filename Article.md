## The Data
Every spreadsheet tells a story, but in our minds it can be hard to truly understand the choices and outcomes by staring at a screen of numbers. After loading a dataset made by CORGIS from a NSDUH study, ranging from 2002 to 2018 that measured substance abuse rates across all 50 states, it showed a digital reflection of public health across the United States. Isolating New York as a primary target of comparison provided a distinct story that revealed that substance abuse is dependent on where you live and how old you are. However, substance abuse is a universal problem that the United States needs to stand up to, regardless of state borders. In the study, alcohol, cigarette, cocaine, marijuana, and tobacco rates were tracked within three distinct age groups: 12 to 17, 18-25, and 26 and up. Across the 17 year span from 2002 through 2018 looking into these various age groups and drug categories, it can be identified how New York compares to the rest of the country.

## The Baseline
Since this analysis uses New York State as a baseline, it is important to look at New York by itself before diving into nationwide statistics.Since the 26 and over age group represents the most significant age range, as it is the adults from 26 and onward, we can look at the rate of substance abuse of various drugs over the years to see how the state has done.

![New York Alcohol 26+](./NYAlcohol26+.png)
Looking at the rate of substance abuse of alcohol over the years, it is clear that from 2008 to 2018 there was a massive decline year over year in abuse of alcohol. This paints a picture that the rest of the country will have a hard time beating. Several factors could have contributed to this decline, with strict public health policies limiting alcohol availability, aggressive promotion of healthier lifestyles, and the rise of non-alcoholic alternatives being key drivers of this rapid change.

![New York Cigarettes 26+](./NYCigarettes26+.png)
When looking into cigarette rates of abuse, we see a similar clear drop year over year, stemming all the way back in 2002. Key drivers of this change may be from a comprehensive strategy by the state with high taxes, strict marketing restrictions, and intensive public health campaigns.

![New York Tobacco 26+](./NYTobacco26+.png)
Similar to cigarettes, tobacco has seen a steady drop over the years. Also similarly, high taxes and strict smoking regulations in public spaces have led to strong changes. Bans on flavored e-cigarettes also contributed. 

![New York Cocaine 26+](./NYCocaine26+.png)
While looking into alcohol and cigarettes showed a lot of promise for the state, looking at the cocaine rate of substance abuse shows a much different story, with the rate climbing since 2010 back to very similar rates from 2005. Some key factors that have led to this increase could be record-breaking coca cultivation in Colombia that has led to a flood of the product and increased affordability from that, fentanyl contamination leading to accidental addiction, and distribution networks, with New York City remaining a hub for trafficking. Mental health issues and high levels of depression could also have led to such behaviors.

![New York Marijuana 26+](./NYMarijuana26+.png)
Looking into marijuana, we see an increase, with the highest being from 2017 to 2018. This seems unsurprising, as increased accessibility and legalization has led to much higher usage. And high-potency products have assisted with increased substance abuse. With these results in mind, from alcohol, cigarettes and tobacco's success stories to cocaine and marijuana's steady increases, we can keep these data points in mind as we move to comparisons nationwide.


## The Youth: Ages 12 to 17
Taking in data from this NSDUH study, we get substance abuse rates across the country, spanning all the states, and across many age groups. One of such age groups was from ages 12 to 17. When looking into how New York State compared to all other states, it told quietly that New York sat comfortably below the national average. 

![US National Substance Abuse Rates Over Time Ages 12-17](./National12to17.png)
By taking the mean of all other state's substance abuses for ages 12-17, then comparing it to New York State, a clear trend emerges. The thick bolded red dash at 1.0 shows New York State as a baseline. It can be seen that across all drug categories, New York State sits at the lowest rate in comparison, with Cigarettes noticeably having the most substantial difference. After examining the youth, will the trends remain consistent as we move through various age groups?

## Young Adults: Ages 18 to 25
![US National Substance Abuse Rates Over Time Ages 18-25](./National18to25.png)
After having analyzed the youth and now looking into young adults, it can be seen that while nationally cigarettes, tobacco and alcohol remained far above New York State's rates, all of a sudden Marijuana moves to below New York's average, and cocaine plummets to .86. This means that from simply going up an age group to young adults, New York State has a large increase in the rate of substance abuse when it comes to marijuana and cocaine compared to the national average.

## Adults: Ages 26 and Up
![US National Substance Abuse Rates Over Time Ages 26+](./National26+.png)
Finally when we reach adults, we can see the big picture of how throughout the age groups New York State compares nationally. What remains most consistent across all the age groups is that New York State sits well below the national substance abuse average for tobacco and cigarettes, yet sits closer and closer to the national average for alcohol. Marijuana drifts back to the national mean, however cocaine continues to drop all the way to .72 in 2018, highlighting that New York State has a higher cocaine substance abuse rate than the national average.

![US National Substance Abuse Rates Over Time](./National_Abuse_Rates.png)
This chart shows for all age groups and substances how the national averages compare to New York State as a baseline. While not the easiest to see which exact lines correlate, it provides an overall picture that generally, New York State tends to have lower substance abuse rates than the national average.

## Conclusions
While in this data analysis New York State was the baseline, which provided useful insight to New York State residents, it does not provide the clearest picture that the United States has been faced with one of the most devastating public health catastrophes of our time, the opioid epidemic. This data analysis does not show that while youth usage rates have been steadily declining, the adult age group has seen catastrophic rates caused by the use of deadly drugs like fentanyl, heroin, cocaine, oxycodone, and more. 
### So what can be done?
For one, there needs to be a pivot from prevention to harm reduction ([US Department of Health & Human Services Harm Reduction](https://www.hhs.gov/overdose-prevention/harm-reduction)). This would include implementing comprehensive HIV services in Syringe Services Program Settings, developing educational materials and programs to reduce stigma, and more. This would also include rapid real-time data sharing. For example, the CDC provides the Syndromic Surveillance (DOSE-SYS) that pulls ecltronic health record data from emergency departments, allowing health departments to quickly identify sudden local spikes in non-fatal overdoses ([CDC DOSE-SYS](https://www.cdc.gov/overdose-prevention/data-research/facts-stats/dose-dashboard-nonfatal-surveillance-data.html)). The CDC also provides the Overdose Data to Action (OD2A) that funds state and local juridictions to link real-time data directly to public health responses([CDC OD2A](https://www.cdc.gov/overdose-prevention/php/od2a/index.html)). Another method that could be implemented is the hub and spoke model ([State of Vermont Blueprint for Health](https://blueprintforhealth.vermont.gov/about-blueprint/hub-and-spoke)). In this model, the hubs provide highly specialized and regional Opioid Treatment Programs that provide intensive daily therapy and medication for complex cases. The spokes provide the local primary care offices and community clinics where waivered doctors and nurse practitioners integrate ongoing maintenance treatment directly into standard healthcare. While these pivots are important, they are far from the whole solution. It is important that policies can stay on pace with the changes seen, which is why it is important to stay informed and that we continue to promote advocacy and funding towards issues that matter; for example, funding to SAMHSA grants that impact local access to lifesavers, where consistent funding is vital to helping issues like this to keep people alive.
